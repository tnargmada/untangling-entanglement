  • Classical
    • How it works
      • Data is stored in bits, which are binary (off or on, 0 or 1)
      • There are a number of actions that you can apply to a bit: switch it to the other value, AND, OR, etc
      • This is implemented with electricity, and this is how your computer works
      • With Tron, the positions of the motorcycles are saved as bits, and where they go next is based on calculations, which are series of gates, performed on these bits.
      • Computers have a lot of bits. To show how many bits you need for stuff, a letter is represented as 8 bits. One second of high-quality MP3 audio is 40KB, which is 40,000 bytes, or 320,000 bits. A 200GB hard drive is 1,600,000,000,000 (1.6 quintillion)
    • Where it fails
      • Computers are really good but they’re not perfect
      • In our modern computers, a bit is stored in a transistor, which is essentially a switch. Now, if there are 1.6 quintillion switches in your computer, these must be pretty small switches
      • The problem is that they are getting too small. We’ve made transistors out of single atoms, and now we can’t really make them smaller for much longer
      • Computers have been getting much more powerful, and we depend on that progress. Now that progress may slow down or stop
    • Some alternatives
    • There are some other ways to make progress that don’t require smaller components
    • Specialized chips
    • Analog computing
    • Reversible computing
    • Quantum computing

  • Quantum: Two main advantages
    • Superposition
      • The bits, rather than being totally 1 or totally zero, can be essentially between the two, some combination of 1 and some combination of zero
      • This allows them to represent multiple states at once, being between states allows you to manipulate both zero and one simultaneously
        • In quantum Tron, the multiple positions of the bike are represented as superpositions of quantum bits
      • However, they can only be measured as zero or one, with a probability of being measured as either
        • This means that if the bit is halfway between zero and one, there is a 50% chance it will be measured as one and 50% that it will be zero
          • 50% chance that the bike is in either position
        • So when you calculate multiple states at once, you only get one answer
          • Your bike actually only went one direction, and you either won or lost in the end
    • Entanglement
      • The state of superposed bits can be dependent on each other
        • Two bits can be between 0 and 1, but if one is 1, the other will be zero and vice versa
      • In Tron, if your bike went through the other bike’s path, your bikes are now entangled
        • If the qubits are measured to say that you crashed at that point, then the other bike will be measured to say that it blocked you
        • If you passed through that point, then the other bike will not have been there.
