    • Quantum Simon Says

    Quantum computing is an extremely confusing topic. I believe that packaging it in the form of a game is the best way to learn about it. Rather than trying to understand every minute detail, which is near impossible, I hope to give you a feel for quantum computing in an accessible and fun way. Games have the power to simplify and abstract an idea, while still maintaining its complexity. Anyone can learn something from a game, whether they are a child or an expert on the topic. This page explains how to play the game Quantum Simon Says, and some of the quantum mechanics behind the game. I recommend downloading the game with the link to the left before reading this guide. If you're interested, you can also check out the github page.


    Download Instructions

    Mac
    This game was written in python. To download the python file, click "download" to the left, then unzip the file. To install the packages that the game uses, open the Terminal app, and type the following command into the terminal, then hit enter:

    $ pip install pygame projectq

    Then to launch the game, type:

    $ python

    and drag the file (quantumsimonsays.py) into terminal.

    Then press enter.


    Game Instructions

    This is the simplest example of a game in Quantum Simon Says. The goal of the game is to highlight only the square that is outlined in black. To do this, you can combine squares diagonally: if the squares are the same color, they will combine upwards, as you see in this example. "W" combines the squares up and to the right; "S" combines the squares up and to the left.

    As you can see, the squares aren't always the same color. If the squares are opposite colors, they combine downwards instead. In this case, "W" combines the squares down and to the left, and "S" combines the squares up and to the right.

    To end the game, you press enter. The goal is to win as quickly as possible. If only one square is highlighted, it will remain highlighted. However, if multiple squares are highlighted, as in the screenshot to the right, only one square will remain highlighted. If a square is brighter (more saturated), it has a higher chance of being highlighted after the game ends. When the game starts, all the squares are equally highlighted, so there is a 25% chance of the correct square being highlighted when you press enter.

    To change the color in the direction of "W" (that is, up and to the right or down and to the left), press "A." To change the color in the direction of "S," press "Q." By changing the colors, you can set up to combine the squares the way you want.

    Quantum-Powered

    This game represents a superposition of quantum bits. There's a deeper explanation of this phenomenon under the "Guide" tab, but I'll give you the summary. Each of the squares in Quantum Simon Says represents a binary number, as you can see on the right. These numbers are represented by two bits. But these bits are special, because they use a quantum property called superposition, which allows them to be between 0 and 1, representing multiple numbers simultaneously. That's why multiple squares can be highlighted at the same time. The colors are called the "phase" of the superposition, and when you combine the superposition you are applying an operation that both creates superpositions and undoes them, depending on the amplitude (the saturation of the square) and the phase. That operation is called a Hadamard gate, and when you press "W" you are applying it to the 1st bit; when you press "S" you are applying it to the 2nd bit. When you press "A," you are changing the phase of the first bit, and when you press "Q" you are changing the phase of the second bit.
